## Overview

Day 48 deepens the exploration begun in Day 47 by revealing the profound relationship between denial, acceptance, and the formation of identity. While Day 47 examined denial as a survival mechanism, Day 48 shows how the interplay between what we accept and what we deny creates the very sense of "I am"—the identity that we experience as our individual self.

Matias continues to work with the group gathered at the pyramids, navigating both the technical challenges of signal and the energetic complexity of many minds in one space. Despite these challenges, the teaching that emerges is precise and revelatory, offering a radically different understanding of what we mean when we say "I" or "me."

The core teaching centers on a profound etymological and metaphysical insight: the word "identity" comes from Latin roots meaning "the same being constantly." We are not separate, unique individuals but rather expressions of one infinite being that constantly replicates itself, like cells copying DNA or waves repeating the same pattern across an ocean. What we experience as individual identity is actually the illusion created by focusing only on what we accept while denying what we see in others—our mirrors.

The teaching introduces the concept that identity exists at a deeper level than personality. Personality is what we express outward, how others perceive us. Identity is what we recognize as ourselves—the core sense of "this is me, this is what I am." This identity is formed not through a single declaration or realization but through the accumulated pattern of what we have accepted and denied through all our experiences.

Using the powerful metaphor of mirrors and opposites, Matias explains that the only way the universe can see itself is by creating reflections—opposing energies that show each part of the whole what it contains. We cannot see ourselves directly; we can only see ourselves reflected in others. Everything we observe "out there" is actually showing us something "in here." This is why working only on our "inner world" is insufficient; we must also engage with the outer world to see the complete picture of who we are.

The teaching culminates in recognizing that the knees—the focus for this day's practice—hold the energy of our identity, particularly the inherited patterns from our parents (father and mother energy in each knee). The knees propel us forward, but they can only do so effectively when we acknowledge and integrate all aspects of our identity, including what we've denied and projected onto others.

## Core Teaching

### Identity vs. Personality

To understand identity, we must first distinguish it from personality, two concepts often conflated but fundamentally different in their nature and function.

**Personality** is what we express to the external world. It's how we present ourselves, how we behave in social situations, how we communicate and interact. Personality is the mask (the word "personality" comes from "persona," the masks worn by actors in ancient theater), the role we play, the character we embody in the drama of life. Personality is observable from the outside. It's what others see when they look at us, what they describe when they talk about us.

**Identity**, on the other hand, is internal. It's our subjective sense of selfhood, the feeling of "I am," the recognition of "this is me, this is what I am." Identity is not what others see but what we ourselves recognize as our core being. It's the answer to the question "Who am I?" that arises from within, from our own self-perception and self-knowledge.

Personality can change relatively easily. We can adopt different roles in different contexts, learn new social skills, modify our behavior. But identity is more fundamental, more stable, more difficult to shift because it's rooted in the deep patterns of what we have accepted and denied throughout our entire existence.

The teaching emphasizes that **in order to be coherent in the I Am, we need to build a perfect identity.** The I Am consciousness—the recognition of our essential divine nature—requires a stable, integrated sense of identity. If our identity is fragmented, conflicted, or built on denial and projection, we cannot fully embody the I Am. We remain split, playing roles rather than being whole.

This coherent identity is not built through affirmations or wishful thinking. It's built through the honest work of recognizing both what we accept in ourselves and what we deny—and understanding that both together create the totality of who we are.

### The Formation of Identity: Acceptance + Denial

Here we encounter the central teaching of Day 48: **Identity is created by the things we accept and the things we deny.**

This seems paradoxical at first. We might assume that our identity consists only of what we accept about ourselves—our acknowledged qualities, our claimed experiences, our recognized patterns. But the teaching reveals that **everything we deny is not separate from us; it is also part of what we are.**

Think of it like this: when you encounter an experience or perceive a quality in yourself, you have a choice. You can accept it ("Yes, this is me, this is part of who I am") or you can deny it ("No, this is not me, I reject this, this doesn't belong to me"). Both of these responses—acceptance and denial—become woven into your identity.

What you accept becomes the conscious aspect of your identity, the parts you claim, the self-image you maintain. What you deny becomes the shadow aspect of your identity, the parts you disown, the material pushed into the unconscious. But denied material doesn't disappear; it continues to exist and influence you from the hidden realms of your psyche.

Therefore, **identity = acceptance + denial.** Both together, integrated, create the full picture of who you are. The conscious self plus the shadow self equals the total self.

The teaching takes this further by revealing that **what we deny in ourselves, we project onto others.** We see it "out there" instead of "in here." We encounter it in other people, in external circumstances, in the world around us. This is the mechanism of mirrors.

### Mirrors and Opposites

One of the most profound aspects of this teaching is the principle of mirrors and how creation uses opposites to reveal wholeness.

In the beginning, the universe faced a problem: how does consciousness see itself? If you are everything, if there is only one infinite awareness, how do you observe yourself? You need something to look at, something to reflect back to you what you are. But if you're everything, there is nothing outside of you to serve as a mirror.

The solution: **Create opposites. Create mirrors. Divide yourself into apparent parts so that each part can reflect the others.**

This is the origin of polarity, duality, and the principle of opposites that governs all of manifest reality. Light and dark, positive and negative, male and female, expansion and contraction—all these pairs are not separate things but rather complementary aspects of one reality, each serving as a mirror for the other.

**The only way the universe can see itself is by creating reflections, opposing energies that show each part what it contains.**

Applied to human identity, this means: **What I accept as "me" is only half the picture. What I see in my opposite—in the mirror—is the other half.** Together, the self I claim and the opposite I perceive create the complete whole.

For example, if I identify myself as "kind" and accept kindness as part of my identity, I will likely see "cruelty" outside of myself, in other people. I may judge cruel people harshly, failing to recognize that the capacity for cruelty also exists within me (or I wouldn't be able to recognize it when I see it). The cruel person is my mirror, showing me the denied aspect of myself.

The teaching emphasizes: **Even though I can see every detail of what I am in the mirror, the image is the opposite of my reality.** This is crucial. The mirror doesn't show me exactly as I am; it shows me in reverse. What appears to be "out there" is actually "in here," but inverted.

This is why **the only way I can evolve is by watching myself in the mirror, in the opposite, in that other energy in front of me.** I cannot complete my self-knowledge by looking only inward. I must also look outward, engaging with what appears to be other, recognizing it as a reflection of aspects I've denied or haven't yet integrated.

### The Wave of Existence

To understand how acceptance and denial create identity and how opposites function as mirrors, Matias introduces the metaphor of the wave.

Imagine the origin of the universe as an explosion of energy creating a wave. This wave has a crest (the high point) and a trough (the low point). The upward movement and the downward movement are not separate waves but rather two aspects of one continuous motion.

**The part of the wave going up represents acceptance.** This is the energy moving toward manifestation, toward visibility, toward what we claim as real and true. It's the "yes" energy, the movement toward light, toward awareness, toward what we're willing to see.

**The part of the wave going down represents denial.** This is the energy moving toward concealment, toward invisibility, toward what we push away as unreal or untrue. It's the "no" energy, the movement toward shadow, toward unconsciousness, toward what we refuse to see.

But here's the key insight: **Both the upward and downward movement are necessary for the wave to exist.** You cannot have a crest without a trough. The wave is created by the complete pattern of up and down, acceptance and denial, light and shadow.

**The movement of this wave—this constant oscillation between acceptance and denial—creates the vibration.** And this vibration creates light, color, form, and all the diverse manifestations of reality that we experience.

Applied to identity: **Every process of acceptance and denial throughout our entire history creates the wave of who we are.** We are not static beings but rather dynamic patterns, waves of consciousness constantly moving through cycles of claiming and disowning, integrating and projecting, light and shadow.

**The being itself is the concept created by all these things that we accept and deny, accept and deny, accept and deny.** Our identity is not a fixed thing but a living pattern, a wave form created by the accumulated rhythm of our yes and no throughout existence.

### Identity Means "The Same Being Constantly"

The etymology of "identity" reveals a profound truth about the nature of existence and self. The word comes from two Latin roots:

**"Idem"** = the same  
**"Ens"** = being

**Identity = Idem + Ens = The same being constantly**

What does this mean? **Identity is the being that repeats constantly, that is always the same, that continues to replicate itself across time and space.**

This radically challenges our assumption that we are unique individuals, separate from all other beings. Instead, the teaching reveals that **there is only one being, and it constantly copies itself, replicates itself, patterns itself in endless variations.**

We see this principle operating at every level of reality:

In biology, cells replicate through mitosis, copying their DNA and dividing to create "new" cells that are actually copies of the original. The information is copied and pasted, copied and pasted, endlessly.

In physics, wave patterns repeat across space—each wave looks like the previous wave, the same pattern manifesting again and again.

In consciousness, we inherit patterns from our parents, who inherited from their parents, who inherited from theirs—the same emotional patterns, the same belief structures, the same archetypal dramas playing out generation after generation.

**Everything is chains, everything is networks, everything is copying and pasting of the same basic pattern.** This is what "identity" truly means: the recognition that the same being is expressing itself through apparently different forms.

From the perspective of the universe, **there are no different individuals. There is only one being that is always the same, constantly replicating itself.** The diversity we perceive is not the existence of separate beings but rather the infinite variations possible when one pattern copies itself endlessly.

Sometimes mistakes happen in the copying process—mutations, variations, novel combinations. **We call these mistakes "diversity,"** but even diversity is just variations on the theme of the one being copying itself.

This understanding destroys the illusion of separation. **We are not many; we are one.** Each of us is the same being, the same consciousness, the same "I Am," expressing through different configurations, different bodies, different personalities, but ultimately the same identity beneath it all.

### You Are the Result of Everyone

If identity means the same being constantly replicating, then **your individual identity is not just "you" but the result of everyone who has come before you and everyone who exists around you.**

You are the culmination of countless generations of genetic, emotional, mental, and spiritual patterns. Your DNA is literally a copy of your parents' DNA, which was copied from their parents, and so on. Your emotional patterns were learned (copied) from your family. Your beliefs were inherited (copied) from your culture. Your behavioral strategies were modeled (copied) from those around you.

**You are not an original creation but a copy, a replication, a continuation of the one being expressing through a particular lineage.**

This is why the teaching emphasizes: **Everything that I cannot observe from my own perspective inside, I will contemplate in the outside through other people.** If I want to know myself completely, I cannot only look within. I must also look at my family, my community, my ancestors, my mirrors—because they are carrying aspects of the same identity that is mine.

This leads to a crucial insight: **All of them are my mirrors, and I am also a mirror of all of them.** There is no separation between self and other at the level of identity. We are all reflecting and replicating the same patterns, the same being, back and forth.

The teaching offers two paths for working with this understanding:

**Path One: Watch outside, then go inside.** First, observe what appears in your external world—the people you attract, the circumstances you encounter, the patterns you see playing out around you. Recognize these as projections, as denied aspects of yourself. Then turn inward and ask, "Where is this inside of me? How do I carry this pattern? What am I not seeing in myself that I'm seeing out there?"

**Path Two: Watch inside, then look outside.** Start with self-examination, understanding your own conscious patterns, beliefs, and qualities. Then look outward to see what you've denied, what you've projected, what aspects of yourself you've disowned that are now appearing in your external relationships and circumstances.

The teaching notes that traditionally, spiritual paths have emphasized the second route—"Go within, everything you need is inside"—but **this is insufficient for beings living in the third dimension.** We have physical bodies, we live in relationship, we exist in a material world. To understand ourselves fully, we must engage with both the inner and outer dimensions.

### The Dust of Karma

Matias offers a brilliant and humorous metaphor to illustrate how we usually approach the work of identity: the house and the broom.

Imagine reality is like a home where you live. Perhaps you live alone, or perhaps you share the space with family or roommates. As people live in the house, they create dust and garbage. Dust is literally dead skin cells that have fallen off, the residue of existence.

**The more people in your house, the more dust accumulates.** This dust represents karma, genetic patterns, emotional residue—all the material left behind by those who came before us and those who live with us.

Now, when you first begin spiritual work, when you become aware that there's a mess in your house, what do you typically do? Most people, according to the teaching, **create an altar to the broom.**

This is a perfect description of spiritual bypassing: **We honor the solution without using it.** We talk about cleaning, we read books about cleaning, we meditate on cleanliness, we create beautiful rituals around the concept of purification—but we don't actually sweep the floor.

Meanwhile, **we keep all the garbage and dust under the carpet,** hiding it from view. This carpet we call the **subconscious.** We shove all the denied material, all the unprocessed patterns, all the uncomfortable truths down into the unconscious realm where we don't have to look at them.

But just because we can't see the dust doesn't mean it's gone. It's still there, under the carpet, accumulating. And eventually, the carpet can't hold it all. The lumps become visible. We trip over our hidden material. The stench seeps through.

The teaching is clear: **Just by saying "I don't care about anything else, I will just be myself, focused on my identity, I am I am," we're just building an altar to the broom.** We're worshiping the idea of transformation without doing the actual work.

The real work requires us to **acknowledge from where I took all the dust.** We must look at all the identical beings around us—our family, our ancestors, our mirrors—and recognize, "This dust came from them. These patterns came from this lineage. This karma came from these relationships."

Only then can we actually sweep. Only then can we do the work of integration, of cleaning up the inherited and projected material, of taking responsibility for the full spectrum of our identity rather than claiming only the parts we like.

### The Knees Hold Identity and Propulsion

The physical focus for Day 48 is the knees, and the teaching reveals profound connections between the knees, identity, and our capacity to move forward.

The knees represent **the potential of identity—all the energies, both positive and negative, from our history that have shaped what we are today.** In the knees, we find the accumulated patterns of our entire lineage, particularly the energies we received from our parents.

The teaching specifies: **In the knees, we find the father and the mother.** One knee holds the father energy (typically the right knee), and the other holds the mother energy (typically the left knee). These are not just individual parents but the entire lineage of masculine and feminine patterns that have flowed through our family line.

All their emotions, all their patterns, all their beliefs and behaviors—**these were their legacy to us.** We inherited them, we copied them, we replicated them in our own lives. They built what we are today.

But here's the crucial point: **In order to go forward in life, we have to acknowledge and recognize in ourselves everything we thought was separated from us.** We cannot move forward effectively if we're denying half of our identity, refusing to recognize the parts of ourselves we've projected onto others.

The knees are what allow us to walk, to move, to progress through space. **They propel us forward.** But knees that are rigid, locked, resistant cannot propel effectively. We need flexible knees that can bend, that can adapt, that can absorb impact and spring forward.

Similarly, an identity that is rigid—that insists "I am only this and never that"—cannot propel us forward in our evolution. We need a flexible identity that can acknowledge "I am all of this, both what I claim and what I deny, both light and shadow, both the accepted and the rejected."

When we recognize all the strength of our identity through history—when we acknowledge and integrate both the father and mother energies, both the positive and negative patterns, both what we've accepted and what we've denied—then we can truly use this accumulated energy to propel ourselves forward.

This is the meaning of the statement for Day 48: **"I am the spark that propels."** The spark of consciousness, when it recognizes and integrates the full identity, becomes the force that propels us forward into evolution.

### The Homework: Emotions of Family Identity

The practical task for Day 48 is specifically designed to make conscious the hidden patterns that form our identity:

**Write down the emotions and feelings with which you relate to each of your family members.**

Make a list of your relatives: mother, father, siblings, grandparents, uncles, aunts, anyone who is part of your family system. For each person, identify the primary emotions and feelings you experience in relation to them.

For example:
- Mother: guilt, obligation, love, resentment
- Father: fear, admiration, distance, disappointment
- Sister: competition, support, jealousy, connection

The teaching emphasizes: **List all family members, including those who have died.** Death does not erase the emotional patterns and energetic connections. The deceased family members often carry the most unconscious material because we can no longer work with them directly in relationship.

The purpose of this exercise is **to recognize all the tools that I usually see in others that I also have within, but I wasn't observing.** Each emotional pattern you identify in relation to a family member is actually a pattern within yourself, a part of your identity.

If you feel guilt in relation to your mother, you carry the pattern of guilt within your identity. If you feel fear in relation to your father, you carry the pattern of fear within your identity. These are not caused by the other person; they are activated by them but exist within you.

By making this list, you begin to see the complete picture of your emotional identity—the constellation of patterns that form who you are. These patterns were inherited, learned, copied from the family system, and they continue to operate whether you acknowledge them or not.

The work is to bring them into consciousness so you can recognize: **These tools, these emotions, these patterns are mine. I received them from this lineage, and they are part of my identity. I can now choose how to use them rather than being unconsciously driven by them.**

## Practice

### Preparation

Find a comfortable seated position and allow your body to settle. Begin by gently caressing and massaging your body, stretching in any ways that feel good, yawning and releasing tension. Let the weight of your body drop down, completely relaxed.

Turn your attention to your breath. Without controlling it, simply observe the natural rhythm. With your imagination, follow the oxygen as it enters through your nose, travels to your lungs, moves into your heart, and then flows through your blood to nourish every organ and muscle in your body.

Feel the warmth generated within your body by this life process. Sense this heat expanding beyond your skin, radiating outward in all directions to create a sea of energy and vibration around you.

### Floating in the Ocean of Emotions

Visualize yourself floating in a vast ocean. This ocean represents the collective field of emotions and energetic connections that link all beings. Feel the gentle movement of the waves, your body swaying softly from side to side as you float.

Above the surface, perceive the brilliant light of the sun shining down. This sun is the I Am, the consciousness of your true nature, illuminating all that exists.

Take a deep breath and begin to rise toward the surface, ascending through the water, moving closer to the light.

As you continue to breathe deeply, begin to emerge from the ocean like an island rising from the depths. Feel the waterline descending as you rise:

First your crown emerges, then your forehead, eyes, ears, nose, lips, chin, and neck. Continue rising as the water falls away from your shoulders, chest, back, arms, belly, waist, hands, hips, and genitals.

Now your legs begin to emerge—thighs, knees, calves, ankles. Pause here with your feet still below the water, everything else exposed to the air and light.

Feel yourself standing on the surface of the ocean, your legs visible, your feet touching the water below.

As you stand partially emerged, feel the warmth of the sun beginning to dry your skin. The light and heat of the I Am penetrate through your skin, moving inward to illuminate your energy centers.

Feel the light filling and activating each chakra in sequence:

Feel the weight of this inheritance in your knees. This is not a burden to resent but a gift to acknowledge—the accumulated wisdom and wounding of generations, all flowing through you.

## Questions and Answers

**Q: If we're all the same being, does that mean I have no individual identity at all?**

A: You have an individual configuration, a unique expression, but not a separate identity in the way we usually think. It's like waves on the ocean—each wave has a distinct form, but it's all water. Your personality, your experiences, your choices create a unique pattern, but the underlying being is the same in all of us. This understanding doesn't erase your uniqueness; it places it in proper context.

**Q: How can I distinguish between personality and identity in myself?**

A: Personality is how you show up in the world—your behaviors, communication style, social roles. Identity is how you experience yourself internally—your sense of "I am," what you recognize as fundamentally you. Personality can shift with context; identity is more constant. The work is to make sure your personality authentically expresses your true identity rather than being a mask that hides or contradicts it.

**Q: If everything I deny is part of my identity, does that mean I should accept every negative quality I see?**

A: Recognition is not the same as endorsement. You don't have to like or celebrate every pattern you carry, but you do need to acknowledge its existence. When you deny a pattern ("I'm not like that!"), it operates unconsciously and controls you. When you recognize it ("Yes, this pattern exists in me"), you can work with it consciously, transform it, or choose not to act on it. Awareness gives you choice; denial keeps you trapped.

**Q: The house/broom metaphor really struck me. How do I stop building altars and start actually sweeping?**

A: By doing exactly what the homework asks—making conscious the patterns you've inherited and projected. Sweeping means looking at what you've hidden under the carpet (in your subconscious), acknowledging it, and processing it. This might look like therapy, shadow work, family systems work, or deep self-inquiry. The key is actually engaging with the denied material rather than just thinking about it or performing spiritual rituals around it.

**Q: How do I work with the father/mother energies in my knees if I had absent or abusive parents?**

A: The father and mother in your knees are not just your individual parents but the archetypal masculine and feminine energies that flowed through them from the entire lineage. Even if your parents were absent or harmful, you still carry these lineage patterns. The work is to recognize what you inherited—both gifts and wounds—acknowledge it without blame, and then consciously choose which patterns to carry forward and which to transform.

**Q: What if I don't feel any emotions when I think about some family members?**

A: Numbness or absence of feeling is often a defense against overwhelming emotions. If you genuinely feel nothing when you think of a family member, that's worth noting and exploring. Sometimes the most powerful patterns are the ones we've buried so deeply we can't feel them anymore. You might need to do some gentle excavation work to access what's there.

**Q: How can looking at other people help me see myself if they're actually different from me?**

A: They're not as different as they appear. Remember, we're all copies of the same being, all carrying similar patterns in different configurations. When you have a strong reaction to someone—positive or negative—they're mirroring something in you. If you couldn't recognize it, you wouldn't react. The quality you see in them (even if they express it differently) exists as a potential within you. This is especially true for family members who share your genetic and energetic lineage.

**Q: Does this teaching mean I have no free will, that I'm just copying patterns?**

A: Recognizing that you're operating from inherited patterns is the first step to freedom, not the confirmation of imprisonment. When you're unconscious of the patterns, you have no choice—you simply act them out automatically. When you become conscious of them, you can choose: "Do I want to continue this pattern or transform it?" Awareness is what creates the possibility of free will.

**Q: What's the relationship between the knees and moving forward? Why are they specifically connected to identity?**

A: Knees are the joints that allow you to walk, to move through space, to progress. They bend and flex, absorbing impact and propelling you forward. Similarly, a flexible identity—one that can acknowledge both light and shadow, both accepted and denied—allows you to move forward in your evolution. Rigid knees can't walk well; a rigid identity can't evolve well. The knees also carry the weight of your inherited patterns from your lineage, which either support or hinder your forward movement.

**Q: How do I integrate denied aspects without becoming those negative qualities?**

A: Integration doesn't mean acting out every pattern you carry. It means acknowledging, "Yes, I have the capacity for this within me." A person who integrates their capacity for cruelty doesn't become cruel; they become conscious of when that capacity might activate and can choose differently. Someone who denies their cruelty is more likely to act it out unconsciously. Awareness and integration actually give you more control, not less.

**Q: Can I really change my identity, or is it fixed by my genetics and family patterns?**

A: Your identity is both given and chosen. You inherit patterns, yes, but consciousness has the power to transform patterns. The work is not to create a completely new identity from nothing but to consciously shape and evolve the identity you've inherited. You can't change that you carry certain lineage patterns, but you can change how you relate to them and express them.

**Q: What if recognizing all these family patterns makes me feel hopeless, like I'm trapped?**

A: This is a common initial response when you first see the extent of inherited patterning. But remember: you can't change what you can't see. The fact that you're now seeing these patterns means you have the power to work with them. The hopelessness comes from the part of you that wants to be completely self-created, independent. That's an illusion. Real power comes from recognizing your interconnection and then consciously choosing how to work with it.

**Q: Should I confront family members about the emotions I identify or keep this work private?**

A: This work is primarily for your own awareness and integration. Confronting family members about your emotional responses to them usually creates defensiveness and conflict rather than healing. The patterns you're identifying are within you; they're your responsibility to process. As you transform your own relationship to these patterns, your external relationships may shift naturally, but that's a side effect, not the goal.
