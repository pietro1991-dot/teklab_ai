## Core Teaching

### What is a Scheme?

The concept of **scheme** comes from the Greek language: **schema**, which means **the shape, the form** of something. It comes from the European language root **"ske"** which means **to hold something, to hold**.

This is why, as humans, we have this preconception of creating a scheme or living our life through a schematic way as something wrong or limiting. We think it's putting limits on the things. But the reason we do that is because of the origin of the word.

**Schema mainly represents something that holds everything in one form.** It puts some limits, yes—but not to restrict, **to contain**. It creates a shape so we can grasp what would otherwise be formless and overwhelming.

### From Text to Scheme: The Learning Process

To understand schemes, we have to go back to school. (I'm sorry for those of you who hated school, but sometimes to understand life we need to go to the main topics.)

In school, the teachers usually gave you a big text with a lot of information and they said, "You have to make a scheme. You have to make a summary of all the information, an outline of what is this topic."

In order to do that, you have to go through a process:

#### Step 1: Summary

First, you make a **summary**. We usually relate summary with putting all that information into a small thing, like taking information and keeping just the important things. But it's not that.

**Summary comes from the word "sum," which is to add things, not to subtract things.** It's totally the opposite.

To make a summary is to **put all the things that were spread in a big text and gather all of it, the heavy of it, into just a few words**. The word summary has **the sum of all the other concepts**—it's not like you take many things so you have just the important things. It's that you take all information into just tiny containers.

#### Step 2: Diagram

In order to get a summary, you need to make a **diagram**.

What is a diagram? **Diagram is a Greek word that comes from "dia" (to go through) and "gram" (to write).** So **diagram means to go through the writing**.

When you read a text that you're trying to understand, in order to keep the storyline, what you have to do is **pick up some words through the text and write them down like in the line of the text**. Those words will be the key to remember the concepts of every paragraph.

So you are **summarizing the concepts of each paragraph into one word**. And through that, you connect the topics of each paragraph to have the whole idea. That's what we call the diagram.

#### Step 3: Scheme

All of that is the **outline** of a story, which we know as the word **scheme**.

**Scheme gives the shape to all information that was spread around.** All this content, this information that was all around, now it's put in a **frame**. That's why we call it a **framework**. It puts some limits on it so it doesn't matter what the structure is—it is a frame. It can be a triangle, a circle, it doesn't matter. The shape doesn't matter, but **it limits the concept**.

### Schemes and Intelligence

The process of getting this scheme helps us to understand the world. Everything that we have around, everything that is spread in the cosmos, is so much data, so much information.

**If we put all of it together in only one concept—collecting all the important things and putting it in just one symbol—we later can, just by seeing that symbol in that frame, understand all the data and information that is behind it without the need of going step by step to see all the things.**

This is why this symbol, this concept, is what gives us the ability to be **intelligent**.

**Intelligence comes from the Latin word "inter-legere."** Inter-legere means **to read in between the lines**. 

**Legere** (the origin of "read") basically means **to collect things**. When you read, you are collecting different concepts and things to yourself. That's the meaning of legere.

When you have **"inter,"** it means **in between**. So when you **collect things in between**, you are reading between the lines.

This speaks about the ability that humans have: **to understand from a big concept just a few ideas, put it together in one symbol, and through the ability to have intelligence, we understand the symbol.**

**That's why humans are different from other animals—because we have this specialty that we can be intelligent, which means to collect ideas and put them on one concept.**

### The Letter A: 4,000 Years in One Symbol

The importance of schemes and of these concepts is that it helps us to understand how the normal things that we use every day have so much weight and so much information that we don't even notice, because we normalize all the things around us.

The example I want to give you today is **the letter A**.

**The letter A, the sound "A," that sound has such a weight of history, all the meanings in that tiny letter that we use every day.** The letter A is the **summary, the scheme of 4,000 years of history**.

#### The Evolution of the Letter A

We all know the letter A like this: **A**. But what happened in history is that the letter A was first positioned differently, and it started to turn and turn until we know it now as A.

- **Egyptian times:** The letter was positioned like this ∀ (upside down from our current A)
- **Phoenician times:** It turned like this ⋀ (starting to rotate)
- **Greek times:** It turned like this Λ (almost there)
- **Roman/Latin times:** It finished turning like this **A** (our current form)

The letter A was **spinning 180 degrees** according to the writings of each culture.

#### The Meaning of A

In Greek times, the letter was called **Alpha**.  
In Phoenician times, it was called **Aleph**.  
In Egyptian times, it was called **Aph** (or **Apis**).

**All of them mean the same thing: BULL. The letter A means bull** and comes from **the head of a bull**.

If you look at the letter A—two lines coming down like horns, one line across like the head—you can see the bull's head.

#### Why is A the First Letter?

Why is A the first letter of the alphabet? Because **in the moment when all the ancient concepts were creating the alphabet, the letter A represented the main divinity of that era.**

The bull was the representation of the god, of the divine, of the sacred. It was the most important animal, the most powerful force. The bull represented strength, fertility, the power of creation.

**So the first letter was the bull—A—because it represented the divine, the origin, the source.**

And now, thousands of years later, you use that letter without even thinking about it. You write "A" and you have no idea you're drawing a bull's head, you're invoking an ancient god, you're carrying forward a symbol that has traveled through Egyptian, Phoenician, Greek, and Latin civilizations.

**That's the power of a scheme. One symbol. 4,000 years. Infinite information. And you can read it, understand it, use it—because you are intelligent. You can read between the lines.**

## Questions for Reflection

### Q: What is the difference between a summary and just picking out important information?

A summary is not about discarding the unimportant—it's about **adding (sum) everything together** into a condensed form. You're not leaving things out; you're compressing them. It's like taking a huge amount of data and zipping it into a file. The data is still all there, just in a smaller container. That's what makes it different from simply selecting highlights.

### Q: How does creating a diagram help me learn?

A diagram forces you to **go through the text and extract key words** that represent each section. This process makes you engage with the material actively rather than passively reading. You're identifying the skeleton of the information—the structure that holds it all together. Once you have the skeleton, you can remember and rebuild the entire concept.

### Q: What does it mean to "read between the lines" (intelligence)?

It means you don't need every single piece of information spelled out for you. You can see a symbol (like the letter A) and understand not just "this makes the 'ah' sound," but also "this represents a bull, divinity, ancient civilizations, the first principle." You collect the visible and the invisible meanings. You read what's written AND what's implied. That's intelligence.

### Q: Why should I care about the history of the letter A?

Because it shows you that **nothing is arbitrary**. Every symbol you use carries weight, history, meaning. When you recognize this, you stop taking things for granted. You start living in a world that's rich with significance rather than flat and meaningless. The letter A is not just a shape—it's a 4,000-year-old scheme containing the spiritual understanding of multiple civilizations. You use it every day. That's profound.

### Q: How can I apply the concept of schemes to my overwhelming life?

When you feel buried in information, tasks, problems—**create a scheme**. Reduce it to one word, one image, one framework. What is the SHAPE of this situation? What is the essential pattern? Once you have the scheme, you can navigate without drowning in details. This is not avoidance—this is intelligence in action.

### Q: What does the ankles chakra have to do with schemes?

The ankles allow you to adapt to changing terrain. Schemes allow your mind to adapt to changing information. Both are about **flexibility within structure**. You need the structure (the scheme, the ankle joint) to move effectively, but you also need the flexibility to adjust as conditions change. Learning is exactly this—structured flexibility.

### Q: If schemes hold so much information, are they always accurate?

Schemes are frameworks—they're useful, but they're not the full reality. The map is not the territory. The letter A is not actually a bull, it's a symbol that represents a bull (which represented divinity). Schemes are tools for comprehension, not perfect replicas. Use them, but don't confuse the container for the content.
