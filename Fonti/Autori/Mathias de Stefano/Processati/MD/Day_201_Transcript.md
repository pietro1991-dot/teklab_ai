## Core Teaching

### The Etymology of "Planet": The Wanderer

The word **"planet"** comes from the Greek **"planētēs"** (πλανήτης), which is composed of two parts:

**1. "Planan"** = to wander, to roam, to go without direction  
**2. "-tēs"** = the one who does the action (like "agent" or "doer")

Put together: **"Planētēs" = "the wanderer" or "the one who wanders."**

In Latin, this became **"planeta,"** and in English, **"planet."**

The word is also related to **"errant"**—which means **"wandering, roaming, making mistakes, going without a clear destination."**

So when ancient people looked at the night sky, they saw:
- **Fixed stars** = lights that stayed in the same position relative to each other, forming constellations
- **Wandering stars** = lights that **moved** through the constellations, night after night, seemingly without a fixed path

These wandering stars were **mysterious**. They didn't follow the same rules as the fixed stars. They moved. They changed position. They seemed to have their own will.

**That's why they called them "planets"—the wanderers, the errant ones.**

The ancients had **no idea** these were other worlds. They thought they were just **stars that moved**—perhaps the gods themselves, walking through the heavens.

### The Ancient Understanding: Gods Walking in the Sky

Because the planets were mysterious and seemed to move with intention, ancient cultures believed they were **gods.**

The **fixed stars** were the realm of the divine order—the unchanging, eternal patterns.

But the **planets** (wandering stars) were the **gods in motion**—deities traveling through the heavens, influencing events on Earth.

That's why planets were named after gods:
- **Mercury** (Hermes) = the messenger
- **Venus** (Aphrodite) = the goddess of love
- **Mars** (Ares) = the god of war
- **Jupiter** (Zeus) = the king of gods
- **Saturn** (Chronos) = the god of time

The ancients could only see **up to Saturn** with the naked eye. The outer planets—**Uranus, Neptune, Pluto**—weren't discovered until much later (18th, 19th, and 20th centuries).

But when modern astronomers discovered them, they **continued the tradition** of naming planets after gods, even though they were scientists, not mystics.

Why? **To honor the ancient way of understanding the cosmos.**

### What IS a Planet? (The Scientific Definition)

Now that we know the **etymology** (wanderer), let's understand what a planet **actually is**:

A **planet** is a celestial body that:

1. **Orbits a star** (in our case, the Sun)
2. **Has enough mass** to form a roughly spherical shape due to its own gravity
3. **Has cleared its orbital path** of other debris (this is why Pluto was reclassified as a "dwarf planet"—it shares its orbit with other objects)

Planets are **NOT stars.** They don't produce their own light. They **reflect** the light of the star they orbit.

### Our Star is Named "Sun"

Here's something important that many people don't realize:

**The Sun is a STAR.**

All the stars you see in the night sky are **not** suns—they're **stars**.

The only star called **"Sun"** is **ours**—the star at the center of our solar system.

If we lived in the **Alpha Centauri system**, we wouldn't call that star "the Sun." We'd call it **"Alpha Centauri"** (or whatever name that civilization gave it).

We call our star **"Sun"** (Sol in Latin), and that's why our system is called the **"Solar System"**—because it belongs to the star named Sun.

**Sun = the name of our specific star.**

### Where is Our Sun Located?

Our Sun is located in the **Milky Way Galaxy**, specifically in one of the spiral arms called the **Orion Arm** (or Orion Spur).

We are **NOT** at the center of the galaxy.

The **center of the Milky Way** contains thousands of stars (and likely a supermassive black hole), but our Sun is about **26,000 light-years away** from the galactic center—out in one of the arms.

### How Planets Form: The Explosion of the Sun

About **4.5 billion years ago**, the Sun was born.

It formed from a **collapsing cloud of gas and dust** in space. As this cloud collapsed under its own gravity, the center became denser and hotter, eventually igniting nuclear fusion—**the Sun was born.**

But not all of the gas and dust fell into the Sun. Much of it was **ejected outward** in the early explosive phases of the Sun's formation.

This created a **disk of material** spinning around the young Sun—gas, dust, minerals, metals, all swirling in orbit.

And then, something amazing happened:

**The particles started to hit each other.**

As they collided, they generated **heat** and **friction**, which created **magnetism**.

These tiny magnetic forces caused particles to **stick together**, forming **clumps**.

Over time, these clumps grew bigger and bigger—**attracting more particles through gravity and magnetism.**

Eventually, these clumps became **rocks**, then **boulders**, then **planetesimals** (small planet-like objects), and finally—after millions of years—**planets.**

**This is how planets form: through magnetism, gravity, and time.**

### The Role of Magnetism and Gravity

Matias emphasizes: **Magnetism is KEY.**

Without magnetism, the particles would just float around forever, never sticking together.

But because the **fast movement** of particles created **magnetic fields**, they began to **attract each other**, forming cores.

Once these cores became large enough, **gravity** took over.

Gravity pulled in more and more material, compacting it into spheres.

The larger the planet, the stronger its gravity, the more material it could pull in.

This is why:
- **Rocky planets** (Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars) are **closer to the Sun**—they formed from heavier, denser materials that didn't get blown away
- **Gas giants** (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) are **farther out**—they formed from lighter gases and ices that couldn't exist close to the Sun's heat

### Planets Are Part of Us

Here's the spiritual teaching embedded in the science:

**We are made of the same dust that formed the planets.**

The **iron** in your blood, the **calcium** in your bones, the **carbon** in your cells—all of it came from the **explosion of stars** (not just our Sun, but previous generations of stars that exploded as supernovae and scattered their elements across space).

**You are stardust. You are planet-matter. You are Solar System material.**

When you look at the planets, you're not looking at "other things out there."

You're looking at **your cosmic siblings**—formed from the same source, spinning in the same system, held by the same gravity.

### Why We Study the Planets

Matias is setting up the framework for the rest of this week:

Each planet represents a **physical influence** on our bodies, our cycles, our rhythms.

The closer the planet, the stronger the influence:
- **The Moon** (closest) = strongest daily/monthly influence
- **The Sun** = life force, vitality, circadian rhythms
- **Inner planets** (Mercury, Venus, Mars) = personal, immediate effects
- **Outer planets** (Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, Neptune) = generational, slower, deeper effects

Understanding **what planets are**—how they formed, why they exist, what they represent—is the foundation for understanding **how they affect us.**

## Questions for Reflection

### Q: What does the word "planet" mean?

**"Planet" comes from Greek "planētēs," meaning "the wanderer" or "the one who wanders without destination."** Ancient people saw planets as **errant stars**—lights that moved through the fixed constellations, seemingly with their own will. They didn't know these were other worlds; they thought they were wandering stars, possibly gods walking in the sky.

### Q: Is the Sun a planet or a star?

**The Sun is a STAR.** It's not "a sun"—**Sun is the name of our specific star**. All the other stars in the galaxy have their own names (Sirius, Alpha Centauri, Betelgeuse, etc.). We call our star "the Sun," and that's why our system is called the **Solar System**—it belongs to the star named Sun.

### Q: Where is our Sun located in the galaxy?

Our Sun is in the **Milky Way Galaxy**, specifically in the **Orion Arm** (or Orion Spur)—one of the spiral arms. We are about **26,000 light-years away from the galactic center.** We are NOT at the center of the galaxy; the center contains thousands of stars and likely a supermassive black hole.

### Q: How did planets form?

About **4.5 billion years ago**, our young Sun formed from a collapsing cloud of gas and dust. As it formed, it **ejected material outward**, creating a spinning disk around it. **Particles in this disk collided**, generating **heat and magnetism**. Magnetism caused particles to **stick together**, forming clumps. Over millions of years, these clumps grew through **gravity and magnetism**, eventually becoming **planets.**

### Q: Why is magnetism important in planet formation?

**Without magnetism, particles would never stick together.** When particles collide at high speed, they generate **magnetic fields** that cause them to **attract each other**.
