## Core Teaching

### The Moon is a Satellite, Not a Planet

First, let's clarify the difference between a **planet** and a **satellite**:

**A planet:**
- Orbits a **star** (like the Sun)
- Has enough mass to form a roughly spherical shape
- Has a strong **magnetic field**
- Has **cleared its orbital path** of debris

**A satellite:**
- Orbits a **planet** (not a star)
- Has a **weaker magnetic field** (not strong enough to orbit a star on its own)
- Is "captured" or formed by the **gravitational pull of a planet**

**The Moon is a SATELLITE**—it orbits **Earth**, not the Sun.

And the **name of our satellite is "Moon"** (or "Luna" in Latin, "Selene" in Greek).

Just like our star is called **"the Sun"** (not "a sun"), our satellite is called **"the Moon"** (not "a moon").

Other planets have satellites too—but they have different names:
- Mars has **Phobos** and **Deimos**
- Jupiter has **Io, Europa, Ganymede, Callisto** (and dozens more)
- Saturn has **Titan, Enceladus, Mimas**, and others

**Those are satellites. But only OURS is called "the Moon."**

### The Moon is the Closest Celestial Body to Earth

The Moon is only about **384,400 kilometers** (238,855 miles) away from Earth.

In cosmic terms, **that's nothing.**

Compare it to:
- The **Sun** is about **150 million kilometers** away
- The nearest **planet** (Venus or Mars, depending on orbits) is millions of kilometers away
- The nearest **star** (Proxima Centauri) is **4.24 light-years** away

**The Moon is RIGHT HERE.**

And because it's so close, its influence on us is **enormous.**

### Light from the Moon Takes Only One Second to Reach Us

Matias emphasizes this:

**The light from the Moon takes only ONE SECOND to travel to Earth.**

Compare that to:
- Light from the **Sun** takes about **8 minutes**
- Light from the nearest **star** takes **over 4 years**

When you look at the Moon, you're seeing it **almost in real-time**—only one second in the past.

**This proximity means the Moon's influence is IMMEDIATE.**

### The Moon Doesn't Have Its Own Light

Like all planets and satellites, **the Moon does not produce its own light.**

The light we see from the Moon is **reflected sunlight.**

Here's how it works:

1. The **Sun** emits light (photons)
2. That light travels to the **Moon**
3. The Moon's surface **reflects** that light
4. The reflected light travels to **Earth** (in one second)
5. We see the **Moon shining**

**What we call "moonlight" is actually SUNLIGHT bouncing off the Moon.**

This is important spiritually because it means:

**The Moon is a REFLECTOR, not a SOURCE.**

The Moon doesn't create—it **receives and reflects.**

(This is why, in many traditions, the Moon is associated with the **feminine principle**—receptive, reflective, cyclical.)

### How the Moon Formed: The Giant Impact Hypothesis

So how DID the Moon form?

The most widely accepted scientific theory is the **Giant Impact Hypothesis** (also called the "Big Splash" or "Theia Impact").

Here's the story:

About **4.5 billion years ago**, when Earth was still young (and looked more like Venus—molten, fiery, chaotic), a **Mars-sized planetoid** (a proto-planet) was wandering through the solar system.

This planetoid is called **Theia** (named after the Greek Titaness, mother of Selene, the Moon goddess).

**Theia was on a collision course with Earth.**

When Theia **crashed into Earth**, the impact was catastrophic:

- Parts of **Theia** exploded into space
- Parts of **Earth's crust and mantle** were blown off into orbit
- The debris formed a **ring of material** around Earth

Over time (millions of years), this debris **coalesced** (came together through gravity and magnetism) to form **the Moon.**

**So the Moon is made of:**
- Material from the **proto-planet Theia**
- Material from **Earth's outer layers**

**The Moon is literally a piece of Earth + a piece of another world, brought together by cosmic collision.**

### Without the Moon, We Would Not Exist

Matias is emphatic about this:

**"Without the Moon, we are all dead. And not only that, without the Moon, we wouldn't have been alive in the first place."**

Why?

**The Moon's gravity is ESSENTIAL for life on Earth.**

Here's what the Moon does:

**1. Stabilizes Earth's Axial Tilt**

Earth is tilted at about **23.5 degrees** on its axis.

This tilt is what gives us **seasons** (summer, winter, spring, fall).

**Without the Moon's gravitational pull, Earth's tilt would wobble wildly**—swinging from 0 degrees to 85 degrees over millions of years.

This would cause **catastrophic climate changes**—ice ages, extreme heat, unpredictable seasons.

**Life as we know it couldn't survive.**

The Moon **stabilizes** Earth's tilt, keeping our climate **relatively stable** over long periods.

**2. Creates the Tides**

The Moon's gravity **pulls on Earth's oceans**, creating the **tides**—the daily rise and fall of sea levels.

**Why are tides important?**

- Tides **mix nutrients** in the ocean, supporting marine life
- Tides create **tidal pools**, which were likely where **early life evolved** (the transition from water to land)
- Tides regulate **coastal ecosystems**

**Without tides, the evolution of life would have been completely different (or might not have happened at all).**

**3. Slows Earth's Rotation**

The Moon's gravitational pull **gradually slows Earth's rotation.**

Billions of years ago, **an Earth day was only about 6 hours long.**

The Moon's influence has **slowed us down** to the current **24-hour day.**

**Why does this matter?**

A 6-hour day would mean:
- Extremely fast winds
- Violent weather
- No time for stable ecosystems to develop

**The Moon gave us TIME—literally.**

**The Moon is not "for us." The Moon is part of the SYSTEM that allowed us to exist.**

## Questions for Reflection

### Q: What's the difference between a planet and a satellite?

**A planet orbits a STAR** (like the Sun), has a strong magnetic field, and has cleared its orbital path. **A satellite orbits a PLANET**, has a weaker magnetic field, and is captured/formed by the planet's gravity. **The Moon is a satellite**—it orbits Earth, not the Sun.
