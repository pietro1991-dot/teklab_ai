## Overview

Day 46 continues the Emotional Week of Virgo with a profound exploration of one of the most misunderstood concepts in spiritual work: manipulation. The very word carries heavy negative connotations in modern consciousness, immediately triggering associations with control, deception, and abuse of power. Yet Matias begins this teaching by stripping away these layers of judgment to reveal the original, neutral meaning of the word—and in doing so, offers a path to reclaiming one of our most fundamental powers.

The word "manipulation" comes from the Latin "manus," meaning hand. To manipulate originally meant simply to do things with your hands, to handle, to create, to make. It is the same root that gives us the word "manifest"—to make a party with your hands, to celebrate through creation. In its purest sense, manipulation is the human capacity to shape reality, to take raw materials and transform them into tools, art, shelter, and sustenance. It is one of the defining characteristics of human evolution.

However, this teaching goes deeper. As humans evolved the capacity for abstract thought, we learned not only to manipulate matter with our hands but also to manipulate ideas with our minds. This mental manipulation is the process of creating concepts, organizing reality into categories, and building the conceptual frameworks that allow us to navigate an infinitely complex world. Here we encounter the double-edged nature of this power: our ability to create concepts is essential for functioning, but it also limits our perception when these concepts become rigid pre-judgments and pre-concepts.

The central revelation of Day 46 is the connection between mental manipulation (how we organize our perception of reality), emotional manipulation (how we unconsciously extract energy from others), and true self-empowerment (learning to manipulate our own energy to become creators of our reality). When we lose the power to generate and manage our own energy—stored and processed in the sacral chakra—we become energetic parasites, unconsciously manipulating the emotions of others to extract the life force we need. The path to liberation is learning to "handle" our own energy, to become conscious creators rather than unconscious consumers.

## Core Teaching

### The Original Meaning of Manipulation

Before we can reclaim the power of manipulation, we must understand what the word truly means. In Latin, "manus" refers to the hand, and words derived from it describe actions performed with the hands. "Manipulation" simply meant to handle something, to work with it, to shape it with your hands. In English, we still have the word "handle" which carries this same meaning—to handle a situation means to deal with it, to manage it, to work with it skillfully.

In the evolution of human consciousness, the development of hand dexterity was revolutionary. Our opposable thumbs and fine motor control allowed us to create tools, build structures, make clothing, prepare food, and shape our environment in ways no other species could. This physical manipulation of the material world is what enabled human civilization to emerge. We took stones and made knives. We took clay and made pots. We took fibers and made cloth. Every act of creation began with our hands manipulating raw materials.

The word "manifest" has the same root: it comes from "manus" (hand) and "festus" (festival or party). To manifest something literally means to make a celebration with your hands, to bring something into physical form through your creative action. When we understand this, we see that manipulation and manifestation are intimately connected—both refer to our capacity to take what exists in potential and make it real through conscious action.

This original meaning contains no judgment, no ethical weight. A potter manipulates clay. A carpenter manipulates wood. A chef manipulates ingredients. A musician manipulates sound. In each case, the word simply describes the act of skillfully working with materials to create something. The negative connotation attached to manipulation in modern language comes from a specific subset of this activity: the manipulation of people, particularly through deception or coercion.

But even this brings us to an important insight: if we can manipulate matter with our hands, and if consciousness evolved from matter, then we also have the capacity to manipulate consciousness with our minds. This is where the teaching becomes profound and challenging.

### Pre-Concepts and Pre-Judgments

To understand how mental manipulation works—and how it both serves and limits us—Matias introduces the concepts of pre-concepts and pre-judgments. These are the mental categories and automatic responses we develop as we learn to navigate reality.

A **pre-concept** is something that has been conceived before your current experience. The word "concept" comes from "conception," which means to give birth to an idea, to originate something in the mind. When you form a concept, you create a mental model of how something is. For example, "the sky is blue" is a concept. It's not objectively true—the sky is not actually blue; it's the scattering of light through the atmosphere that creates the appearance of blue. But we conceive of the sky as blue, and this concept helps us navigate reality.

A pre-concept takes this further: once we have the concept that the sky is blue, we extend it automatically. We pre-conceive that anything wide, celestial, and calming will also be blue. We adjust new experiences to fit our existing concepts without questioning whether they actually match reality. This is useful because it allows us to move through the world quickly, but it also means we're often experiencing our concepts of reality rather than reality itself.

A **pre-judgment** works similarly but involves categorization. The word "judge" comes from the Latin "iudex," meaning to guide or indicate. To judge means to put things in order, to categorize them, to assign them to different boxes. When you judge something, you say, "This belongs in this category. That belongs in that category." This is a neutral, necessary function of consciousness.

Pre-judgment means that once you've placed something in a category based on experience, you automatically place similar things in the same category without examining them individually. You had an experience with fire—it burned you, it caused pain—so you placed "fire" in the "danger/pain" box. Now, whenever you see fire or anything similar to fire, you automatically assign it to the pain category. You pre-judge it.

### The Necessity and Danger of Mental Categories

Matias offers a brilliant example to illustrate how pre-concepts and pre-judgments function: colors. We don't think about colors consciously. We see something and instantly categorize it—"blue," "red," "green"—without examining the actual wavelength of light or considering the subtle variations. There's a box in our mind labeled "blue," and every shade of blue gets tossed into that box: navy, cyan, sky blue, royal blue, all of them simply "blue."

This mental efficiency is essential. If we had to consciously analyze and experience every perception fresh, without any categories or concepts, we would be paralyzed. Imagine if every time you saw your mother, you had to figure out from scratch who she was. Instead, there's a box labeled "mom," and when you see her, you instantly recognize her. The same applies to concepts like "love," "home," "food," "danger," and countless others.

Our brains create these boxes to save energy and processing time. Without them, we couldn't function. This is the evolutionary advantage of pre-concepts and pre-judgments: they free our conscious attention to focus on new challenges rather than re-analyzing everything we already know.

However, there's a profound danger in this system. When we live entirely from our pre-judgments and pre-concepts, we stop experiencing reality directly. We experience only our mental models of reality. We see what we expect to see, filtered through our existing categories. We miss the potential, the novelty, the unexpected possibilities in every moment.

The fire example illustrates this perfectly. A child sees fire for the first time and finds it beautiful, fascinating. Naturally, the child wants to touch it. The child touches the fire and gets burned. The neurological system immediately creates a connection: fire = pain. Fire gets placed in the "danger" box, stored in the "pain" category.

From that moment on, every time the child sees fire, the pre-judgment activates: "Danger! Pain! Stay away!" This is useful for survival. But if the child never goes beyond this pre-judgment, they will never discover all the other potentials of fire: warmth, cooking, light, transformation, medicine, metallurgy, and countless other uses that are essential to human civilization.

The same principle applies to every area of life. If we judge people, situations, and experiences only through our pre-existing categories, we never discover their full potential. We live in a tiny box of our own making, unable to grow or evolve.

### Conscious Judgment vs. Unconscious Pre-Judgment

This teaching makes a crucial distinction that transforms our understanding of spiritual principles. Many spiritual teachings tell us, "Don't judge others. Judgment is bad. Be non-judgmental." But Matias clarifies: **judgment itself is not the problem; pre-judgment is.**

Judgment is the necessary, conscious act of discernment—putting things in their proper place, understanding their nature and function, organizing reality so we can interact with it skillfully. This is essential for living as a conscious being in a complex world. We need to judge situations to know how to respond. We need to judge people to know whether we can trust them. We need to judge information to know whether it's true or false.

The problem is **pre-judgment**, which means reacting based on past experience without fresh perception. Pre-judgment is unconscious; it happens automatically. When we're operating from pre-judgment, we're not truly present. We're not responding to what is; we're reacting to what was.

Here's the key insight: **unconscious beings are moved by pre-judgment; conscious beings are moved by judgment.**

An unconscious person sees a situation and immediately reacts based on their past experiences, their conditioning, their categories. They never actually perceive the situation freshly. They see through the filter of their pre-concepts and pre-judgments.

A conscious person sees the situation, recognizes their pre-judgments arising, and then chooses to look freshly, to discern what is actually happening here and now. They use judgment—conscious discernment—rather than being used by pre-judgment.

This is one of the primary practices of awakening: learning to recognize when you're operating from pre-judgment (reaction) versus judgment (conscious response). It requires constant vigilance and self-awareness.

### The Trap of Spiritual Pre-Judgment

Ironically, many people on the spiritual path fall into a specific trap related to pre-judgment. They hear that "judgment is bad" and that "everything is one," so they swing to the opposite extreme: they try to eliminate all discernment. They say, "I don't judge anything. Everything is equally valid. Who am I to say what's good or bad?"

This might sound spiritually advanced, but it's actually another form of unconsciousness. It's a pre-judgment against judgment itself. The person has placed "judgment" in the "bad/ego/unspiritual" box and now refuses to use this essential faculty of consciousness.

The result is a kind of spiritual passivity or false neutrality. Without proper judgment, we can't navigate reality skillfully. We can't discern truth from lies, health from toxicity, wisdom from delusion. We become vulnerable to manipulation by others precisely because we've disabled our own faculty of discernment.

Matias offers the middle path: we need judgment, but we need to be aware when our judgment has calcified into pre-judgment. The goal is not to eliminate categorization and discernment but to keep them fresh, flexible, and responsive to actual experience rather than simply repeating past patterns.

When we judge consciously, we're not saying "this is good and that is bad" in an absolute sense. We're saying, "This serves this purpose in this context. That serves that purpose in that context." We're organizing reality to understand it, not to control it or make it conform to our preferences.

### Losing Power and Becoming Parasites

The teaching now makes a critical shift from mental manipulation (concepts and judgments) to energetic manipulation (how we interact with others at the level of life force). This is where we discover why manipulation has become such a negative word.

The sacral chakra, located below the navel, is the center of creative energy in the body. This is where we store and process the energy we extract from food, where sexuality resides, where our capacity for creation—both physical and energetic—is held. When we have full access to this power, we can create our own reality. We can "manipulate" our own energy—handle it, direct it, use it to manifest what we choose.

But when we lose this power—when we become disconnected from our sacral energy—we face a crisis. We still need energy to function, but we can no longer generate it ourselves. So we do what all organisms do when they can't produce what they need: we become parasites. We begin to take energy from others.

How do we extract energy from others? Through the emotional links we explored in Day 45. We manipulate the emotions of others to get them to give us the energy we lack. This is emotional manipulation in the negative sense—using tactics, behaviors, and psychological strategies to extract what we need from others without their conscious consent.

This happens unconsciously in most cases. We're not deliberately scheming to drain people's energy. We simply feel a need, and we've learned—often in childhood—certain behaviors that get others to give us attention, validation, sympathy, or care. We make sad faces when we want hugs. We create drama when we want attention. We play victim when we want to be rescued. We use guilt, shame, anger, or seduction to move energy in our direction.

The teaching is uncompromising: **all of us do this.** It's not something only "bad people" do. It's a natural consequence of living in a network of emotional connections while being disconnected from our own power source.

### The Three Steps to Empowerment

Matias offers a practical, step-by-step path to reclaiming our power and ending the cycle of emotional parasitism. Using the simple example of needing a hug, he illustrates how we can transform from unconscious manipulation to conscious empowerment.

**Step One: Recognize the Manipulation**

The first step is becoming aware of when and how you manipulate others emotionally. This requires brutal honesty with yourself. You feel a need—maybe you want affection, attention, validation, or support. Instead of acknowledging the need directly, you create a situation designed to get someone to give you what you want without asking.

For example, you want a hug. Instead of asking for one, you make a sad face, sigh heavily, slouch your shoulders, and wait for someone to notice and offer a hug. This is manipulation. It's indirect, it's unconscious, and it puts the other person in a position where they feel obligated to respond to your unspoken need.

The first step is simply to identify: *Which emotions do I manipulate in others? With whom do I most often do this?* Write these down. Become conscious of your patterns. There's no judgment here, only awareness.

**Step Two: Ask Directly**

The second step is revolutionary in its simplicity: when you need something, **ask for it directly.**

Instead of making a sad face and waiting for someone to offer a hug, you go to them and say, "Can you give me a hug? I need one right now."

This completely changes the dynamic. You're no longer manipulating their emotions to extract what you need. You're making a clear, honest request. They can say yes or no. There's no guilt, no obligation, no game. It's clean, respectful communication.

This step is where you begin to take back your power. You're still asking for something external, but you're doing it consciously and directly. You're being honest about your needs instead of hiding them behind manipulative behaviors.

Many people find this step incredibly difficult at first. It requires vulnerability to state your needs plainly. It requires courage to risk rejection. But it's also incredibly freeing. When you ask directly and someone responds, you know their response is genuine, not extracted through manipulation.

**Step Three: Give to Yourself**

The third step is the culmination of empowerment: **when you need something, give it to yourself.**

In the example of the hug, this means that when you feel the need for a hug, you hug yourself. Wrap your arms around your own body. Hold yourself. Give yourself the comfort and affection you seek.

At first, this might seem absurd or insufficient. We're conditioned to believe that self-soothing is inferior to receiving from others, that giving ourselves what we need is somehow selfish or insufficient. But the body doesn't know the difference. Research shows that self-touch activates the same neurological pathways as receiving touch from others. The comfort is real.

More importantly, this act is profoundly empowering. When you can give yourself what you need, you are no longer at the mercy of others. You're no longer dependent on their availability, their willingness, or their capacity. You become self-sufficient.

This is what it means to "manipulate your own energy"—to handle it, to direct it, to use it to meet your own needs. When you reach this stage, you have reclaimed your creative power. You are generating your own energy rather than parasitically extracting it from others.

### You Are the Creator of Your Reality

Everyone has heard the phrase "You are the creator of your own reality." It's become a cliché in spiritual circles. But most people who affirm this discover it doesn't seem to work. They say the words, they try to visualize what they want, but their reality doesn't change. Why?

Because **you cannot create your reality if you don't know how to handle your own energy.**

Creation requires energy. Manifestation requires power. If your power is scattered, if you're constantly leaking energy through emotional dependencies, if you're spending your life force trying to extract what you need from others, you have nothing left to create with.

The sacral chakra is the seat of creative power. It's where sexual energy, life force, and the capacity for manifestation reside. When this chakra is functioning properly—when you know how to manipulate (handle) your own energy—you can direct that power toward creating the life you choose.

But when this chakra is depleted or when you're disconnected from it, you become dependent. You look outside yourself for everything: for validation, for purpose, for energy, for love. And as long as you're looking outside, you cannot create from within.

The path of empowerment is learning to manage your own energy. This doesn't happen overnight. It requires practice, awareness, and the gradual building of new patterns. But each time you choose to give yourself what you need rather than manipulating others to give it to you, you strengthen your creative power. You become more capable of manifesting your vision.

### Where Is Your Power?

A profound teaching emerges here about the nature of power and victimhood. When people begin to awaken and realize they've lost their power, they often go through a phase of blame. They open the mental "box" labeled "power" and find it empty. Where did the power go?

Their pre-judgment immediately provides an answer: someone took it. The government took it. The church took it. The Illuminati took it. The system took it. Parents took it. Society took it.

This creates a narrative of victimhood in which external forces have stolen what rightfully belongs to you. And if they stole it, then the only way to get it back is to fight them, to blame them, to demand they return what they took.

But this is a trap. It's another form of giving your power away, this time to the story of victimhood itself.

The teaching is clear: **When you lose power, it's not because someone took it from you. It's because you don't know how to handle your own energy, and you've been losing it without even realizing it.**

Power cannot be taken; it can only be given away. And we give it away constantly, unconsciously, every time we look outside ourselves for what we can only find within, every time we manipulate others emotionally to fill our emptiness, every time we believe we need something external to make us whole.

The systems, institutions, and structures that seem to control us only have power because we're collectively giving it to them. We've created a massive network of energetic dependency in which everyone is trying to extract power from everyone else. This creates a pyramid structure where those at the top appear to have all the power, but they're actually just better at manipulating the energy flow.

The real revolution is not fighting the system but reclaiming your power at the individual level. When you know how to handle your own energy, when you can nourish yourself from within, when you stop participating in parasitic exchanges, the entire system loses its grip on you.

### We Created the Systems

This leads to one of the most challenging teachings: the systems we rail against—governments, corporations, religious institutions, all the structures of control—were created by us. Not by "them," by **us**.

These systems emerged from the collective unconscious pattern of energetic parasitism. When large groups of people are all trying to extract energy from each other, hierarchical structures naturally emerge to organize and manage the energy flow. Those systems then perpetuate themselves, teaching each new generation the same patterns of dependency and manipulation.

This doesn't mean there aren't individuals and groups who consciously exploit these systems for their own benefit. There are. But blaming them misses the point. The systems exist because we collectively create and maintain them through our own unconscious participation.

When we wake up and stop feeding the system with our energy, when we reclaim our power to create from within, the system loses its foundation. We don't have to fight it or destroy it. We simply stop sustaining it with our participation.

This is perhaps the most empowering and most challenging aspect of the teaching: you are not a victim of the system; you are a participant in it. And as a participant, you have the power to withdraw your participation and create something new.

## Questions and Answers

**Q: If manipulation is neutral, why does the word have such negative connotations?**

A: The word became negative because the most visible form of manipulation in human society is the manipulation of people, particularly through deception and coercion. When someone manipulates another person to extract what they want without honest communication, it violates consent and creates harm. However, this is a specific misuse of the broader capacity. Reclaiming the original meaning helps us see that the problem is not manipulation itself but unconscious, parasitic manipulation of others.

**Q: How can I tell if I'm using conscious judgment or unconscious pre-judgment?**

A: Ask yourself: Am I responding to what's actually happening right now, or am I reacting based on past experiences? Conscious judgment feels spacious, thoughtful, and present.
