## Overview

Day 47 explores one of the most fundamental patterns that shapes human consciousness: denial. This teaching, delivered with some difficulty as Matias adjusts to the energies of 25 people gathered with him at the pyramids, reveals denial not as a character flaw or psychological problem but as a biological imperative rooted in the very structure of existence itself. From atoms to galaxies, all of creation operates on the principle of seeking stability and conserving energy—and denial is one of the primary mechanisms by which this occurs.

The teaching begins at the most fundamental level of physical reality: atoms and molecules seeking energetic balance. Every particle in the universe is trying to earn and conserve energy because energy is required for two essential life processes: transformation (metamorphosis) and reproduction (transcendence through offspring). Nature teaches organisms to hold their form constant, to resist change, to stay the same because changing requires energy expenditure that could be used for survival and reproduction instead.

This creates what we call the "comfort zone"—not a psychological concept but a biological reality. To conform (to hold the same form constantly) is to stay safe, stable, and energetically efficient. Anything that threatens to change us triggers an automatic response: denial. We push it away, reject it, refuse to acknowledge it because our deepest biological programming says that change equals danger, and danger could mean death.

However, human beings are not meant to simply survive; we are meant to evolve. Evolution requires that we venture beyond the comfort zone, that we expose ourselves to new environments, that we acquire new tools and capacities. The very things we deny—the challenges, the discomforts, the aspects of ourselves we refuse to recognize—are precisely what we need to grow. This creates the central paradox of human existence: our survival mechanisms actively work against our evolutionary potential.

Matias distinguishes between two types of denial: denial by not liking (rejecting what disgusts or threatens us) and denial by not recognizing (refusing to acknowledge aspects of ourselves that others can see). Both serve the same function—protecting our current form from change—but both also trap us in patterns that prevent growth. The path forward is not to eliminate denial but to become conscious of it, to ask "why?" instead of automatically saying "no," and to recognize that everything we deny holds the key to our evolution.

## Core Teaching

### The Universal Principle of Energy Conservation

Before we can understand denial in human psychology and spirituality, we must understand its foundation in the very fabric of existence. Matias takes us to the atomic level, to the basic building blocks of reality, to show us that denial is not a uniquely human problem but a universal pattern woven into the structure of creation itself.

Every atom in existence is seeking energetic balance. An atom with low energy seeks to connect with an atom with high energy so they can share and find equilibrium. When atoms bond together to form molecules, they create structures that are more stable than either atom alone. This stability means they can conserve energy—they don't have to constantly search for what they need because they've found a configuration that works.

This principle scales up through all levels of reality. Molecules bond to form compounds. Compounds organize into cells. Cells form tissues, tissues form organs, organs form organisms. At every level, the organizing principle is the same: find a stable configuration and maintain it because maintaining stability requires less energy than constant change.

Why is energy conservation so crucial? Because all living things have two fundamental imperatives: to transform themselves (metamorphosis, evolution, growth) and to reproduce themselves (create offspring, pass on genetic information, transcend individual death). Both of these processes require enormous amounts of energy. Therefore, organisms instinctively hoard energy, saving it for these crucial moments.

This is why nature programs all living things to resist change. Change requires energy expenditure. If an organism is constantly changing in response to every new stimulus, it will deplete its energy reserves and have nothing left for transformation and reproduction when the time comes. So the biological imperative is clear: maintain your current form, resist change, conserve energy for the moments that matter most.

This is not a mistake or a flaw in nature's design. It's a brilliant survival strategy that has allowed life to persist and diversify across billions of years. The problem arises only when this survival strategy becomes the entire purpose of existence, when preservation replaces evolution, when staying the same becomes more important than becoming what we could be.

### Stability, Comfort Zones, and the Fear of Change

The drive for energy conservation manifests in living beings as two closely related but distinct patterns: stability and conformity to form.

**Stability** is the ability to stay in the same spot, the same place, the same situation constantly. A stable system doesn't rock, doesn't shift, doesn't move. It has found its equilibrium and maintains it. In physics, we call this homeostasis—the tendency of a system to maintain internal stability even when external conditions change.

**Conformity to form** (or "conformation") is the tendency to hold the same shape constantly, to resist any alteration in structure or identity. This is what we commonly call the "comfort zone"—the familiar patterns of thought, behavior, relationship, and experience that define who we believe ourselves to be.

Both stability and conformity serve the same purpose: they minimize energy expenditure and protect the organism from threats. If I stay in the same place (stability) and maintain the same form (conformity), I know what to expect. I've adapted to this environment. I have the tools I need to survive here. Change introduces uncertainty, and uncertainty requires new energy to navigate.

This is why our biology constantly whispers to us: **Don't change.** Every time we contemplate a transformation—leaving a relationship, changing careers, moving to a new place, challenging old beliefs, adopting new practices—we feel resistance. This resistance is not weakness or cowardice; it's the voice of our survival programming saying, "If you change, we might die."

The fear of change is not irrational from the biological perspective. Change does carry risk. When an organism ventures into a new environment, it faces unknown dangers. When it tries a new behavior, it might fail. When it transforms itself, it becomes vulnerable during the transition. These are real risks, and nature has programmed us to take them seriously.

But here's the crucial insight: **what makes sense for pure survival does not make sense for conscious evolution.** A human being is not just a biological organism trying to survive and reproduce. We are consciousness itself, incarnated in physical form, here to evolve, to grow, to discover the full range of our potential. For this purpose, the very strategy that ensures biological survival—resisting change—becomes the primary obstacle.

### The Two Forms of Denial

Matias identifies two primary manifestations of the denial mechanism in human consciousness, both serving the function of protecting our current form from unwanted change.

**Denial by Not Liking** is the most obvious form. We encounter something in the world—a person, an idea, a situation, a teaching—and we immediately feel aversion, disgust, or discomfort. Our response is automatic: "No, I don't like this. I don't want this. This is not for me. Go away."

This type of denial is a protective mechanism. Whatever we're encountering threatens to destabilize our current understanding of reality or challenge our current way of being. Our system interprets this as a survival threat and activates the rejection response. We push the thing away before it can affect us, before it can change us.

For example, if someone challenges our political beliefs, we might immediately deny the validity of their perspective ("That's wrong, that's stupid, I don't want to hear it"). The real threat is not the political idea itself but the possibility that we might have to change our beliefs, which would destabilize our identity and our position in our social group. Denial protects us from this destabilization.

**Denial by Not Recognizing** is more subtle. This occurs when someone tells us something about ourselves—"You are this" or "You do that"—and our immediate response is "No, I'm not. I don't do that." We refuse to recognize in ourselves something that others can clearly see.

This form of denial is particularly insidious because it creates a blind spot. The aspect of ourselves that we don't recognize doesn't disappear; it simply operates unconsciously. We act out the pattern while simultaneously denying that we're doing it. This creates cognitive dissonance and prevents self-awareness.

For example, someone might point out that we're being controlling in a relationship. Our immediate response is "No, I'm not controlling! I'm just trying to help!" We genuinely don't recognize the controlling behavior because acknowledging it would threaten our self-image as a kind, supportive person. The denial protects our self-concept but prevents us from seeing and changing the pattern.

Both forms of denial—by not liking and by not recognizing—serve the same function: they protect our current form from change. They say, "I am fine the way I am. I don't need to change. That thing you're showing me is not part of me, and I refuse to integrate it."

### Why We Say "No" Before We Ask "Why"

One of the most profound observations in this teaching is that **before asking "why," we always say "no" first.** This is the automatic nature of the denial response. When something challenges our stability or threatens our form, we don't pause to investigate, to inquire, to understand. We immediately reject.

This happens because the denial response is faster than conscious thought. It's a survival mechanism hardwired into the nervous system, operating at the level of instinct and reflex. By the time our conscious mind becomes aware of the situation, the "no" has already been activated.

This creates a significant problem for those who seek to expand their consciousness. Expansion requires openness to new information, new experiences, new perspectives. But if our default response to anything new is "no," we're trapped in a closed loop. We can only perceive and integrate what we already know and accept. Everything else gets rejected before we even examine it.

The teaching points to a crucial distinction: **those who first say "no" are those who close themselves and deny, wanting to survive but not to evolve.** Survival and evolution require different strategies. Survival says, "Protect what you have, don't risk change." Evolution says, "Explore what you could become, embrace change as the path to growth."

The question that emerges is this: If survival and evolution are both natural processes, and if denial serves survival, why should we do anything differently? Why not just survive like every other species on the planet?

The answer comes back to human purpose. We are not meant to simply survive. We are consciousness in the process of awakening to itself, and awakening requires that we move beyond the biological programming of survival. We must learn to pause before the automatic "no," to create space for the question "why?" to emerge, to investigate our denials rather than simply acting them out.

When someone shows us something we don't want to see, instead of immediately denying it, we can ask: "Why am I resisting this? What am I protecting? What would change if I acknowledged this truth?" These questions transform denial from an unconscious reflex into a doorway for self-discovery.

### Evolution Requires Leaving the Envelope

Matias introduces several powerful metaphors to illustrate the process of evolution and how it differs from mere survival. One of the most striking is the image of envelopes.

Our body, our personality, our beliefs, our identities—all of these are like envelopes that contain us. They give us form, definition, boundaries. They protect what's inside and separate us from what's outside. These envelopes are necessary; without them, we would have no individual existence.

But **evolution requires taking off the envelopes.** The word "development" literally means to remove the envelopes (from "de-" meaning removal and "veloppe" from Latin "voloper" meaning to wrap or enclose). To develop is to unwrap ourselves, to expose ourselves to new environments and experiences, to venture beyond the protective boundaries we've constructed.

This is terrifying from the survival perspective. The envelopes protect us. Removing them makes us vulnerable. But this vulnerability is precisely what allows growth to occur.

Similarly, the word **"evolution" means to go out and start to make turns** (from "e-" meaning out and "volvere" meaning to roll or turn). Evolution is not a linear progression; it's a spiraling outward, a rolling into new territories, a continuous process of venturing beyond what we know.

And the word **"learn" means to acquire something new** (from Old English "leornian," to get knowledge, to acquire). Learning is not about confirming what we already know; it's about taking in what we don't know, integrating what is foreign to our current understanding.

All of these processes—development, evolution, learning—require that we accept change, that we welcome what is new and unfamiliar, that we allow ourselves to be transformed by exposure to what exists beyond our current boundaries.

Those who remain in their envelopes, who refuse to venture out, who deny everything that threatens to change them, remain static. They survive, perhaps, but they don't grow. They repeat the same patterns, live the same experiences, and eventually, they fossilize.

Those who have the courage to remove the envelopes, to roll outward into unknown territory, to acquire new knowledge and integrate new experiences—these are the ones who evolve. They transform. They become more than they were. And this, according to the teaching, is the true purpose of human existence.

### What You Deny Is What You Need

Here we arrive at perhaps the most challenging and liberating insight of the entire teaching: **Everything you deny is exactly what you need for your evolution.**

This is counterintuitive because our denial mechanism exists precisely to protect us from what we perceive as harmful or threatening. When we deny something, we genuinely believe we're protecting ourselves from something bad, something that would hurt us or diminish us.

But the teaching reveals the opposite: what we deny is not our enemy but our teacher. The things we push away, the aspects of ourselves we refuse to recognize, the experiences we avoid—these are not obstacles to our growth; they are the very substance of our growth.

Why is this true? Because denial only activates when something threatens to change our current form. If we're already comfortable with something, if it fits within our existing structure, we have no need to deny it. Denial arises specifically in response to what could transform us.

Therefore, by mapping our denials, we create a perfect roadmap of our evolutionary edge. Each "no" points to a potential area of growth. Each thing we refuse to look at holds a tool we need to develop. Each person or experience we push away carries a lesson essential for our next stage of development.

This is why **those who become aware of themselves start by acknowledging what they deny.** Self-awareness does not begin with cataloging what we already know about ourselves; it begins with shining light into the shadows, investigating what we've refused to see.

The teaching emphasizes that **denial is not something to get rid of; it's something to become aware of and transform into a tool.** Nature wastes nothing; everything recycles. In the same way, every denial, every resistance, every "no" contains energy that can be transformed into fuel for evolution.

When we recognize what we're denying, we gain access to new tools. We discover capacities we didn't know we had. We integrate aspects of ourselves that were operating unconsciously. We become more whole, more complete, more capable.

But this requires a fundamental shift in how we relate to discomfort. Instead of seeing discomfort as a sign that something is wrong, we must learn to see it as a sign that growth is available. Instead of running from what makes us insecure, we must turn toward it and ask, "What am I being called to learn here? What tools am I being asked to develop?"

### Insecurity Reveals Lack of Tools

One of the most practical insights in this teaching relates to the experience of insecurity. When something outside of us triggers insecurity within us, what does that mean?

The teaching is clear: **Insecurity arises when we encounter something for which we don't have tools.** We feel secure in areas where we've developed skills, knowledge, and capacities. We feel insecure in areas where we lack these resources.

For example, if someone criticizes your work and you feel deeply threatened, it's not because the criticism is necessarily valid or destructive. It's because you haven't yet developed the tools to receive criticism without your self-worth being shaken. Someone who has developed these tools can hear the same criticism and simply evaluate whether it's useful feedback without feeling attacked.

This reframes insecurity from a character flaw to a diagnostic tool. When you feel insecure, instead of judging yourself as weak or inadequate, you can ask: "What tools do I need to develop to navigate this situation with confidence?"

The denial response often activates when we feel insecure. Rather than acknowledging that we lack tools and choosing to develop them (which would require effort and vulnerability), we deny that the situation matters or that we need those tools. We push away the challenge before it can expose our inadequacy.

But this strategy keeps us trapped. If we deny every situation that reveals our lack of tools, we never develop new capacities. We remain limited to the narrow range of experiences we've already mastered.

The path of conscious evolution is different: when insecurity arises, we recognize it as an invitation to grow. We say, "I don't have the tools for this yet, but I'm willing to develop them." We turn toward the challenge rather than away from it. We accept our current limitations while simultaneously committing to transcend them.

This is the difference between those who close themselves (denying what makes them insecure) and those who expand (using insecurity as a compass pointing toward growth).

### The Expansion of Consciousness

Throughout this teaching, Matias repeatedly emphasizes that we are in a phase of human evolution focused on **the expansion of consciousness.** This is not the same as biological evolution, which operates through random mutation and natural selection over millions of years. Conscious evolution is intentional, accelerated, and driven by choice rather than chance.

For beings committed to expanding consciousness, the old survival strategy of denial becomes the primary obstacle. You cannot expand consciousness while simultaneously defending the boundaries of your current awareness. Expansion requires that you constantly challenge and transcend your existing limits.

This doesn't mean biological survival becomes irrelevant. We still live in bodies that require food, shelter, and safety. But **survival can no longer be the primary organizing principle of our lives.** When we prioritize survival above all else, we remain trapped in patterns of fear, scarcity, and self-protection that prevent the very expansion we're here to experience.

The teaching asks a profound question: If all other beings in nature have evolved through denial and self-protection, through maintaining form and resisting change, **why should we do it differently?**

The answer: **Because we are not unconscious animals simply following biological programming. We are consciousness becoming aware of itself, and that awareness carries with it both the capacity and the responsibility to choose a different path.**

Other species evolve unconsciously, over vast spans of time, through random variations and environmental pressures. Humans have the unique capacity to evolve consciously, in our own lifetimes, through intentional practice and deliberate transformation. But this requires that we work directly with the very mechanisms that in other species serve survival—like denial—and transform them into tools for conscious growth.

Those who are willing to accept what they're denying, who are willing to develop the tools they lack, who are willing to venture beyond the comfort zone into unknown territory—these are the ones who contribute to the expansion of consciousness. They become evolution in action, demonstrating that transformation is possible within a single lifetime, that we are not slaves to our conditioning, that awareness itself is the force that liberates.

### The Practice of Asking "Why"

The practical application of this teaching can be summed up in a simple practice: **When you notice yourself saying "no," pause and ask "why."**

This sounds deceptively simple, but it's actually revolutionary. The automatic "no" is unconscious; it bypasses our awareness and operates as pure reflex. By inserting the question "why?" between the stimulus and the response, we create a gap—a moment of consciousness—in which choice becomes possible.

When someone says something you don't like, instead of immediately rejecting it, ask yourself: "Why don't I like this? What in me is threatened by this? What would change if I accepted this?"

When someone points out something about you that you don't recognize, instead of immediately denying it, ask yourself: "Why can't I see this? What purpose does it serve to keep this hidden? What would happen if I acknowledged this truth?"

When you encounter a situation that makes you uncomfortable or insecure, instead of immediately avoiding it, ask yourself: "Why am I uncomfortable? What tools do I lack here? What am I being called to develop?"

These questions transform denial from an unconscious defense mechanism into a tool for self-discovery. They allow you to use your resistances as a map, following them back to the core beliefs, fears, and limitations that shape your life.

Matias acknowledges that this practice can seem exhausting. "But that's exhausting! I'd have to question everything constantly!" Yes, in the beginning, it requires effort and attention. But with practice, it becomes natural. The questioning mind becomes your default mode rather than the denying mind.

And here's the profound truth that makes this effort worthwhile: **While you don't question the causes, you will be living the consequences.** If you don't investigate why you deny, why you react, why you resist, you will simply continue acting out the same patterns, creating the same results, living the same limitations over and over again.

But when you question the causes—when you investigate your denials, examine your resistances, understand your fears—you gain the power to change the consequences. You step out of reaction and into response. You become conscious rather than unconscious. You become the author of your life rather than a character acting out a script written by your conditioning.

### Unconditional Presence and Neutrality

The fruit of working with denial in this conscious way is the development of what Matias calls **unconditional presence** and **neutrality.** These are qualities that arise naturally when we stop being controlled by our automatic denials and acceptances.

Unconditional presence means being fully here, fully available to what is, without needing it to be different than it is. It doesn't mean you don't have preferences or boundaries; it means your presence isn't conditional on getting what you want.

Neutrality means not being thrown off balance by every experience, not oscillating wildly between acceptance and rejection, attraction and aversion. It means having the capacity to observe clearly, to discern wisely, and to respond appropriately without being reactively controlled by circumstances.

These qualities are essential for moving into higher levels of consciousness. You cannot access expanded awareness while being constantly reactive, while being jerked around by every stimulus that triggers your survival programming. The expansion of consciousness requires a stable platform of awareness that can remain present regardless of what arises.

This doesn't mean becoming cold, detached, or unfeeling. True neutrality is warm, alive, and deeply engaged. It's the ability to feel fully without being controlled by feelings, to care deeply without being destroyed by loss, to engage passionately without being attached to outcomes.

The only way to develop this capacity is to work directly with the mechanisms of denial and acceptance that normally operate unconsciously. By bringing awareness to these patterns, by questioning rather than reacting, by using our denials as doorways to growth, we gradually develop the inner stability that allows for true expansion.

## Integration

**Homework:** Write down the biggest denials you have in your life.

Be completely honest with yourself. This is an act of courage, not an exercise in self-judgment. Look at all areas of your life and ask:

**What do I refuse to see or acknowledge?**

Consider both types of denial:

**Denial by not liking:** What situations, ideas, people, or experiences do I automatically push away because I don't like them? What makes me uncomfortable or disgusted? What do I refuse to engage with?

**Denial by not recognizing:** What do people tell me about myself that I refuse to accept? What patterns do others see in me that I insist aren't there? What aspects of my behavior or personality do I minimize or explain away?

As you write, notice what emotions arise. Resistance, defensiveness, shame, fear—all of these are natural responses when we begin to examine our denials. Don't judge these feelings; simply observe them. They're part of the protective mechanism you're learning to work with consciously.

Once you have your list, sit with it. For each denial, ask yourself:

- Why do I deny this?
- What am I protecting by keeping this out of my awareness?
- What would change if I acknowledged this?
- What tools would I need to develop to work with this consciously?

Remember the teaching: everything you deny is exactly what you need for your evolution. Your list is not a catalog of your failures; it's a treasure map pointing toward your next stage of growth.

## Questions and Answers

**Q: If denial is natural and serves survival, why should I fight against it?**

A: You shouldn't fight against it; you should become conscious of it. Fighting denial with force just creates more resistance. The practice is to observe denial when it arises, to question it ("Why am I saying no to this?"), and to gradually develop the capacity to choose rather than simply react. You're not eliminating a natural function; you're bringing it under conscious direction.

**Q: How can I tell the difference between healthy boundaries and denial?**

A: Healthy boundaries come from conscious discernment; denial comes from unconscious fear. When you set a boundary consciously—"This doesn't serve me, so I'm choosing not to engage with it"—you remain open and aware even as you decline. When you're in denial, you refuse to even look at what you're rejecting. You can't discern what you won't examine. The key is: are you choosing from awareness or reacting from fear?

**Q: What if the things I deny really are bad for me? Shouldn't I protect myself?**

A: There's a difference between acknowledging something exists and inviting it into your life. You can recognize a toxic person or situation without denying its reality. In fact, you can only truly protect yourself from something when you see it clearly. Denial doesn't protect you; it creates blind spots that make you more vulnerable. Conscious awareness allows for genuine protection.

**Q: I've tried to face my denials before and it was overwhelming. How do I do this without falling apart?**

A: Go slowly and be gentle with yourself. You don't have to face everything at once. Start with smaller denials, things that feel manageable. Develop tools gradually. Build your capacity over time. And remember: the goal is not to force yourself to accept everything; it's to bring consciousness to your automatic reactions. Even recognizing "I'm denying this and I'm not ready to face it yet" is progress.

**Q: How do I know what tools I need to develop?**

A: Your insecurity shows you. Whatever makes you feel insecure points to an area where you lack tools. If criticism makes you defensive, you need tools for receiving feedback without your self-worth being threatened. If change makes you anxious, you need tools for navigating uncertainty. If conflict makes you shut down, you need tools for staying present during disagreement. The situations that trigger you are teaching you what you need to learn.

**Q: Does this mean I should never say no to anything?**

A: Not at all. Conscious "no" is different from unconscious denial. You will still say no to many things—but from choice rather than fear, from discernment rather than reaction. The practice is to make sure your "no" comes after you've actually examined what you're declining, not before. Ask "why?" first, then decide. Your "no" will be more powerful when it's conscious.

**Q: What if I genuinely can't see something about myself that others keep pointing out?**

A: This is denial by not recognizing, and it's the trickiest form because by definition, you can't see it. The practice here is humility: if multiple people independently point out the same thing, consider the possibility that they're seeing something real, even if you can't see it yet. Ask them for specific examples. Journal about it. Work with a therapist or coach. Sometimes we need help from outside to see our blind spots.

**Q: How long does it take to transform denial patterns?**

A: It varies greatly depending on how deeply rooted the patterns are and how consistently you practice awareness. Some denials release quickly once you see them; others take years of patient work. The key is that every moment of consciousness—every time you catch yourself saying "no" and ask "why?"—weakens the automatic pattern and strengthens your capacity for choice. Change happens gradually, then suddenly.

**Q: What's the connection between denial and the kundalini?**

A: Kundalini is evolutionary energy stored at the root. When all your energy goes into maintaining your current form and denying change, the kundalini remains dormant. When you begin to work consciously with your denials, accepting transformation, you release energy that was locked in resistance. This freed energy becomes available for the kundalini to rise, igniting your evolutionary potential. Denial keeps you static; accepting what you've denied allows you to transform.

**Q: Can working with denial be dangerous? What if I uncover something I can't handle?**

A: This is a legitimate concern, which is why the teaching emphasizes going gradually and developing tools as you go. If you uncover trauma or deeply painful material, seek support from qualified helpers—therapists, healers, teachers. The work of consciousness is not meant to be done in isolation. And remember: you will only uncover what you're ready to handle. Your psyche has its own protective wisdom.

**Q: How do I practice this in daily life when I'm busy and stressed?**

A: Start small. When you notice a strong "no" reaction during your day—to a person, a request, a suggestion—pause for just a moment and ask, "Why am I reacting this way?" That's it. You don't have to do deep analysis in the moment. Just inserting that brief question begins to create the gap between stimulus and response. Over time, this becomes natural, and you'll find you're reacting less and responding more.

**Q: What should I do with my list of denials once I've written it?**

A: Use it as a working document for your evolution. Pick one denial to work with. Explore it deeply: Why do you deny it? What would change if you accepted it? What tools would you need? Then begin practicing with that one pattern. When you've made progress, move to the next. Your list is a curriculum for your growth, showing you exactly what you need to learn next.
